Denarius

The silver denarius was the centerpiece and backbone of the Roman economy for centuries, and this was certainly true during the time of Faustina the Younger. The denarius was ranged in between 18 and 20 mm in diameter and by Faustina’s time, were struck in an alloy of about 83.5% silver with the balance being copper. A denarius of Faustina’s day was valued at sixteen assses, eight duponidii or four sestertii. The denarius is nothing short of an icon in the numismatic and economic worlds. The denarius gave its name to the words for money in Spanish, Portuguese, Italian and Slovene. Another legacy of the denarius is the dinar currencies from many Islamic countries. The denarius is by far the most common coinage denomination found for Faustina the Younger and they feature a large variety of beautiful designs. Many of Faustina’s denarii also survive in excellent states of preservation. A decent denarius of Faustina the Younger can be purchased for as little as $40 USD, which makes them an excellent starting point for aspiring collectors of ancient coinage. On the other side of things, several of the denarii of Faustina the Younger’s posthumous series are quite rare and bring strong prices. There is truly something for everyone in Faustina the Younger denarii.
