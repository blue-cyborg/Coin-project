Aureus

Quinarius Aureus-The gold quinarius aureus was an unusual and tiny (between 13 and 16 mm in diameter) coin that was valued at half an aureus, of about 6 and ¼ denarii. This denomination was only issued for Faustina the Younger on a couple of occasion in all of the decades that her coins were struck. This is easily the scarcest type for Faustina the Younger, though when they are found, they are among the more affordable examples of this tiny, strange coin as the mintages were robust, at least in the terms of quinarius aurei.

Aureus-The gold aureus was the same diameter (between 18 and 20 mm) as the silver denarius and often there would be aurei and denarii struck from the same dies. The aureus was valued at 12 and ½ silver denarii and represented a serious chunk of cash for a Roman living in Faustina’s time. As such, the aureus was typically used for large transactions outside of the borders of the Roman Empire’s core and to pay soldiers on campaign. Any aureus is going to require a serious outlay of cash from the collector, but the aurei of Faustina the Younger are some gorgeous coins, often equaling her silver denarii in beauty. But it’s a beauty all its own. Following the trend of Faustina’s other coins, many of her aurei were minted in large numbers and many survive today, so they are among the more available and affordable coins of this denomination. What is interesting, though, is that the aurei of Faustina the Younger seem to go down in availability as she gets older, meaning that while it’s relatively easy to find aurei with Faustina’s younger depictions on them, it’s somewhat challenging to find aurei from later in her life.
